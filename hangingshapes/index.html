Hanging_Shapes

About

"Hanging Shapes" was my first game project, where players strategically touch parts of a chain to break it and make two shapes collide. This project was a significant milestone in my journey as a game developer, teaching me fundamental concepts like physics interactions, collision mechanics, and player input handling. It was a rewarding experience that laid the foundation for my skills in Unity and C#.

Project Info

 Role: Gameplay Programmer
 Team Size: 1
 Time frame: 1 Week
 Engine: Unity (C#)
Hanging Shapes was my first game, where players strategically break chains to make two shapes collide. It introduced me to essential game development skills, from designing mechanics to implementing UI and optimizing performance. I spent a week creating the game and a day optimizing it for Android and web platforms.
 
• Chain Mechanism and Physics
Chains were built using interconnected GameObjects with hinge joints for realistic swinging and movement. Gravity, mass, and drag values were fine-tuned to simulate natural behavior, while collision detection ensured smooth interactions. Unity's physics engine was configured to maintain performance and accuracy.

• Breaking Chains
The gameplay revolved around breaking specific chain links when touched. Scripts disabled the joints connecting links, allowing the remaining chain to move dynamically. This interaction added complexity and variety to puzzles.












• Shape Collision
The primary objective in Hanging Shapes was to make two shapes collide, which acted as the core win condition of the game. The mechanics of this interaction were carefully designed and implemented for an engaging player experience.

- Physics-Driven Movement:
Each shape was a Rigidbody, allowing it to move naturally under the influence
of Unity's physics system. Gravity ensured that the shapes fell or swung
realistically once the chains holding them were broken.

- Collision Detection:
Unity’s collision detection system was used to identify when the shapes touched
each other. Continuous collision detection was enabled to prevent fast-moving
shapes from bypassing each other due to physics inaccuracies.

- Trigger Zones:
A trigger zone or a collision event script was added to register when the shapes made contact. This setup ensured that the win condition was reliably recognized regardless of how or where the shapes collided.


• Optimization
Performance was key for smooth gameplay on multiple platforms. Adjusting physics settings, reusing GameObjects, and thorough testing helped maintain quality across devices.

• Learnings
This project taught me about physics-based gameplay, working with joints, and optimizing for different platforms. It laid the groundwork for my skills, inspiring confidence to tackle more complex projects in the future.
